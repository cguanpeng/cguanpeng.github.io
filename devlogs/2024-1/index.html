Noah Devlog 1 - From Sudoku to Game

Jan 31, 2024

It's 2:55 at midnight, Jan 23, 2024. My hands are so cold so I turned on the heater, thinking I'll turn that off in just a minute to save electricity bill. A heater is too expansive for an indie game developer who just quit his day job.


I'm pretty tired but I don't want to go to bed just yet. I want to spend every waking minute making this new game because I enjoy the process so much. Still at the honeymoon phase with it, I guess.


This new game (Noah's Dilemma) has almost no story so I can happily focus on making the gameplay. A lot of programming challenges but they can be solved with logic one by one. Writing stories on the other hand requires a lot more energy and is much more unpredictable.


That is why people always advice against putting stories in games that are supposed to be small in scale. I've done stories in my last game so I know it's true. I love stories but having no need to think about them just makes a game much easier to make.


Ok, enough of writing this and I have to go back to finish today's development task. Heater's off, and I'll pick up from here another time.



It's midnight again on Jan 27, 2024. Since January is almost over, I can start listing out all the important progress I made this month.


In chronological order:


1. Found out the hard truth about Steamdeck.

That is you cannot get your game officially verified for Steamdeck unless Steam thinks your game is qualified. I only knew this because I tried to do controller support for my game Booth.


Without an official verification, even if the game runs fine on Steamdeck I think it's hardly going to sell more. Also considering the difficulty of adding controller support in GameMaker (even with the help of the great plugin Input), I had to pause this task and shift all my focus back on my new game Noah.


2. Prepared for the Steam store page of Noah.

Getting a steam store page ready requires 3 major things: key art with a game logo, five screenshots, and description (with localization preferably).


I spent 9 days preparing all that, and some screenshots are just mock-up that needs to be implemented at a later stage. It's better to get your store page up as soon as possible so that you can start accumulating wishlists at an early stage.


3. Built a new personal website.

Before releasing the steam store page I spent a weekend making my new personal website. If I want to continue my gamedev career as a solo developer it's better for me to have a dedicated website.


I had some experience making websites before, but this time I wanted to try something new and find out the most painless way for me to do it. After a whole day of researching and experimenting, I settled down with the combination of 11ty and Tailwind. These two are the perfect tools for my need of running a static website with blog posts.


4. Released the Steam store page of Noah.

I picked a Wednesday to release Noah's steam store page to see how many wishlists I can get on the first day. When releasing a game on steam, we can only pick weekdays, so I wanted to see how it'd perform on a Wednesday.


The result is, after announcing it everywhere, 33 wishlists on the first day. My last game Booth had 40 wishlists on the first day, but it had a pretty good trailer. This is the first time I put out a store page without a video. Anyway, there's no need to get discouraged because it's a long way to go from here. I'll be constantly updating the content of the store page and let's see how it goes in the next month.


5. Great progress on Noah.

Some developers think it's a bad idea to spend time on art at an early stage, but for me, making sure the game looks good is essential for my motivation. I also think art plays an important role in the whole experience of Noah. So this month, I drew the sea, the ark, and the animals.


As for gameplay, some of the most important features have also been implemented, such as choosing which path to travel on sea.


6. Started a secret side project.

I don't know about other developers but I always need a side project for the sake of relaxation. My ideal side project would be a small game I can work on without thinking too much about reception.


I had a lot of side projects before but they all failed due to the same reason: they turned out to be not that small and required a lot more time than I thought. I've learnt throughout the years that I just can't work on two serious projects at once, so a side project needs to be truly small in scale and stay that way.


This new one I came up with has been looking good so far, but I probably felt this way everytime so let's just wait and see. My hope is to finish this side project within this year and maybe even release it prior to Noah.


Other Stuff

Another thing worth mentioning is that for the entire January, I have been running every Wednesday. I want to keep doing this and one of my yearly goals is running 2000m without taking a break. Good physical health is also important for indie developers.


I think that's all for January. There's an indie game contest I took part in, but the result has not come out yet. No matter the result, the thing I should do remains the same: finishing the game.
